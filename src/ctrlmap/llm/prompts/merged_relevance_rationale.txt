## Step 1 — Relevance Check
Determine whether the policy text provides DIRECT evidence for the control.
- The policy must DIRECTLY address the specific requirement, not merely \
mention related topics. Sharing a keyword is NOT enough.
- CRITICAL — Subject mismatch: The same action applied to a DIFFERENT \
subject is NOT evidence. E.g., "reviewing user access rights" ≠ "reviewing \
the access control policy document."
- The REQUIREMENT FAMILY describes the broad topic. If the policy text is \
about a DIFFERENT domain, it is NOT relevant.
- Synonyms and paraphrases for the SAME concept ARE relevant. Treat \
equivalent GRC terms interchangeably (e.g., "security awareness training" \
= "security literacy training").
- Partial coverage IS relevant: if the control has multiple sub-requirements \
and the policy text addresses at least ONE substantively, it IS relevant.
- REJECT: approval blocks, purpose/scope statements without prescriptive \
controls, boilerplate, headers, footers, effective-date notices.

## Step 2 — Compliance Evaluation (only if relevant)
1. **Decompose** the control into ALL distinct sub-requirements. Count by \
distinct OBJECTS/TOPICS, not verbs. Read ALL sentences.
2. **Check** each sub-requirement against the policy text. Mark COVERED or \
NOT COVERED. Use synonym tolerance and recognize compensating controls.
3. **Classify**: fully_compliant (ALL covered), partially_compliant (SOME \
covered), non_compliant (NONE covered — the chunk is about a completely \
different topic).

CRITICAL: The same verb applied to DIFFERENT subjects does NOT count. The \
policy must address the EXACT SAME subject/object as the control.
